Today was the first meeting of the new season, and as the rookies filed into the lab, I handed each of them a small, blank pocket notebook. Some looked confused—after all, we’re a robotics team, not an English class. But I realized that for a student to become a "maker," they first have to become an "observer." 

## The Barrier to Entry
Robotics can be intimidating. You walk into a room filled with aluminum extrusions, lithium batteries, and students arguing about PID loops. It’s easy for a rookie to feel like a "user" of the robot rather than a "builder" of it. They wait for instructions, afraid to touch the hardware for fear of breaking a $500 control hub.

I realized that the notebook is the lowest-stakes piece of hardware in the room. You can't "break" a notebook. You can only fill it. 

## Journaling as a Technical Skill
I’ve modeled this after my own practice. My notebooks are held together by electrical tape and sweat, but they contain the DNA of every project I’ve ever finished. By giving the rookies their own notebooks, I’m inviting them into that same "Culture of Expression."
- **Capture:** I want them to sketch the mechanisms they don't understand yet.
- **Reflection:** I want them to write down one thing they learned and one thing that frustrated them at the end of every meeting.
- **Identity:** When they pull that notebook out of their pocket, they aren't just "students"; they are researchers.

## Mentorship through Observation
Giving a rookie a notebook is a way of saying, *"Your perspective matters."* I’ve found that the students who take the best notes are often the ones who notice the "minor" issues before they become "major" failures. They notice the Dyneema rope starting to fray or the battery cable that’s positioned a bit too close to a moving gear.

It appears likely that the tactile nature of writing helps them "own" the knowledge. Intellectual hesitation may suggest that a digital app is more efficient, but in the heat of a build session, there is no substitute for the speed of a pen on paper.

## Building the Vision
My goal as Captain is to let others see the vision and come up with their own goals that align with it. I don’t want a team of followers; I want a team of designers. The notebook is the first tool I give them to help them find their own voice in the lab.

In the end, we aren't just building robots; we’re building people. And sometimes, the best way to build a person is to give them a place to put their thoughts.
