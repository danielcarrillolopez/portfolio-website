Today I caught myself practicing my eye contact in the mirror and remembered why: **Battle of the Saints**. Standing on a stage at Maryville University, presenting a product I had researched, designed, and prototyped entirely on my own, was one of the most intense moments of my engineering career. It was the moment **Synapse** stopped being a hobby and started being a mission.

## The Shift from Builder to Founder
For months, Synapse was just a problem I couldn't stop thinking about. I was head-deep in **ESP32-C3** wiring and haptic motor calibration. I was comfortable at the workbench. But to get to that stage, I had to learn a completely different set of skills. I had to learn what it meant to run a startup.

I realized that being a "founder" isn't just about having a cool prototype. It’s about building the infrastructure around that prototype:
- **User Validation:** I moved from assuming I knew the problem to conducting real interviews and surveys.
- **Brand Identity:** I designed flyers, a business model canvas, and a public-facing website.
- **The Narrative:** I had to translate high-pitched mechanical jargon into a story about safety and human dignity.

## The Maryville Moment
During the pitch, I explained how Synapse could help someone who is deaf receive a smoke alarm alert through touch. I saw something shift in the room. The judges weren't looking at the CAD models on the screen anymore; they were looking at the human impact. 

That’s when it hit me: **Technology only matters when it advocates for people.** 

It appears likely that the pressure of the competition was the "final test" for the project. Intellectual hesitation made me worry if my 5th-generation prototype was "ready" enough, but nuance suggested that the story was more important than the technical perfection. 

## Failing Forward to the Stage
Standing there, I thought about all the failed parts I’d tossed in the bin over the last few months. Every one of those failures was a "conversation" that led me to that stage. I wasn't just presenting a device; I was presenting the results of a thousand small setbacks that I refused to let stop me.

## Final Reflection
This experience confirmed that the technology I want to build for the rest of my life—assistive, human-centered, and accessible—is worth fighting for. I learned that you only truly rise if you bring others along for the journey, and sometimes, that means stepping off the workbench and onto the stage.

**Tasks & Questions:**
- [x] Refine the Synapse Business Model Canvas based on judge feedback.
- [ ] How can I apply this "narrative engineering" to our next robotics design review?
- [ ] Schedule follow-up research with the deaf and hard-of-hearing advocacy groups I connected with.

In the end, engineering is 50% technical and 50% communication. If you can't tell the story of why your device matters, it stays in the lab.
