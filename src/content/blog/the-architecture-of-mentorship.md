Today I was looking through my people map in Obsidian, and I realized that my most important technical "components" aren't microcontrollers or sensors—they are people. For a long time, I took pride in being a "DIY" engineer. I taught myself CAD, Python, and 3D printing because I had to. But my time at **SLU’s CHROME Lab** and my leadership in **Force Green Robotics** taught me that you can only go so far on your own. 

True engineering maturity begins when you learn how to be mentored.

## The Facilitator: Dixie Baker
Every system needs an entry point. For my professional journey, that was **Dixie Baker** and the **Rockwood Partners in Education (PIE)** program. Dixie didn't just find me an internship; she challenged me to think about my professional identity. She was the one who first told me to *"Focus on the majors in the majors,"* a phrase that has since become the benchmark for every design decision I make. 

## The Technical Guide: Jake Little
At SLU, I worked one-on-one with **Jake Little**. Jake was more than a technical advisor; he was a bridge between my high school robotics experience and the world of formal academic research. He pushed me to iterate on the **Blind Hockey Puck** enclosure not just until it worked, but until it was *rigorous*. He taught me that in research, the "failures" are just as valuable as the "successes" because they define the boundaries of what is possible.

## The Visionary: Dr. Jenna Gorlewicz
Working under **Dr. Gorlewicz** shifted my entire engineering philosophy. She runs the CHROME Lab with a mission to "engineer for the benefit of society." Seeing her manage an interdisciplinary lab—where haptics, robotics, and human-centered design collide—showed me what high-level technical leadership looks like. It’s not just about the machine; it’s about the human impact.

## Why Mentorship is a System
I’ve realized that a mentor network is like a control system. 
- **The Input:** My curiosity and DIY drive.
- **The Controller:** My mentors, who provide the feedback loops to keep me from "oscillating" or wasting energy on the "minors."
- **The Output:** A more resilient, people-centered approach to engineering.

It appears likely that without these connections, I would still be building cool things in my room, but I wouldn't be building solutions for the community. Nuance suggests that "Self-Made" is a myth in engineering. We are all built by the people who were willing to share their time, their failures, and their "5th grade language" with us.

**Reflections:**
- [x] Send a follow-up update to the SLU team on my current Synapse progress.
- [ ] How can I provide this same "entry point" for the rookies on Force Green?
- [ ] Mentorship isn't a one-way street; what technical value am I bringing back to the lab?

In the end, we rise by bringing others along. If you want to build the future, you have to be willing to listen to the people who have already been there.
