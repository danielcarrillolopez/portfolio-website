# The Sleep Satisfaction Audit: Why I’m Building Synapse

I realized today that I am a morning person who absolutely hates mornings. It’s a strange contradiction. I love the idea of the "early start," but the reality of waking up usually involves me hitting the snooze button six times and feeling like I’ve been hit by a truck. While analyzing my sleep data this week, I decided to stop complaining and start engineering.

## The Data Discovery
I’ve been keeping a "Sleep Satisfaction Log" in my Obsidian vault. I tracked three variables: Sleep Duration, Alarm Type, and Morning Mood. The results were startling. On days where I used a loud, auditory alarm, my mood was consistently lower, and my "Morning Inertia" (the time it takes to actually get out of bed) was over 45 minutes.

### The Problem with Sound
Auditory alarms are inherently stressful. They trigger a "Fight or Flight" response. For someone like me, who is a heavy sleeper, I eventually learned to integrate the alarm into my dreams—rendering it useless. It may suggest that our bodies aren't designed to be screamed at by a smartphone speaker at 6:30 AM.

## The Haptic Pivot
While working on a different project involving vibration motors, I had a realization: What if the alarm was a physical sensation rather than a sound? This was the "Phase 0" of **Synapse**. 

### Human-Centered User Research
I started researching how the skin perceives tactile information. Unlike the ears, which can "tune out" background noise, the skin is incredibly sensitive to sudden physical changes. By using a wearable haptic band, I could create a "Silent Alarm"—a gentle tap on the wrist that wakes the user without waking their partner or stressing their nervous system.

## Nuance & Hesitation
There is a potential downside here: "Sensory Habituation." If the vibration is always the same, will my body eventually ignore it too? I’m likely to need a system that varies the haptic patterns—sometimes a rhythmic pulse, sometimes a sharp burst—to keep the brain from getting too comfortable.

Building Synapse isn't just about building a product; it’s about solving a problem that affects my daily quality of life. It’s about making technology that respects the biological reality of our bodies.
