Today I sat down with a motor driver that wouldn't stop oscillating, and it hit me: my own life is doing the exact same thing. I’ve been reading about **General Systems Theory**, and it’s shifted how I view my "problems." In engineering, we’re taught linear causality—*A causes B, so fix A*. But in the human system, everything is a loop.

## The Cake and the System
Your therapist or mentor might tell you that your actions don't work in straight lines—they work in circles. While reviewing my notes on systems, I kept coming back to the "cake" metaphor. You can't understand a cake by just looking at an egg or a bag of flour. The whole is greater than the sum of its parts. I realized that I’ve been trying to "fix" my life by looking at the "eggs" (coffee, naps, lists) instead of looking at the **cake** (the entire self-maintaining cycle of my day).

## Circular Causality: The Sleep Trap
In linear thinking, you'd ask: "What's the cause of my sleep problem?" and try to fix that one thing. But in circular causality, there is no single cause. The problem is the entire loop maintaining itself.

My sleep isn't a "problem to be solved"—it’s a loop maintaining itself. It may suggest that my "solutions" are actually the fuel. 
- **The Loop:** Tired during the day -> Take a "strategic" nap -> Surge of energy at 11 PM -> Work because I feel like I'm falling behind -> Can't sleep at 1 AM -> Loop Closed.

It appears likely that by trying to "intervene" at the end of the loop with coffee or list-making, I’m just reinforcing the stability of the system. In systems theory, this is **Negative Feedback**—not "bad" feedback, but feedback that resists change and maintains the status quo (Homeostasis).

## Feedback Loops and Homeostasis
Feedback loops occur when outputs of a system return as inputs. 
- **Negative Feedback Loops (Morphostasis):** These patterns resist change. My family system might unconsciously push back if I try to be "less productive" because it disrupts the established balance. Even dysfunctional patterns feel "safe" because they are familiar.
- **Positive Feedback Loops (Morphogenesis):** These amplify change. Practice a skill -> Get better -> Feel motivated -> Practice more. This is how we grow, but it can also lead to destructive spirals if not managed.

I’ve realized that my current system is too weighted toward *morphostasis*—stability-seeking. To get to *morphogenesis*—actual growth—I have to be willing to let the system become chaotic for a moment.

## The Productivity Loop
My productivity cycle is a classic system:
1.  **Belief:** My worth depends on what I produce.
2.  **Feeling:** Anxious when not doing something.
3.  **Action:** Work constantly, avoid "unproductive" people.
4.  **Outcome:** Feel temporarily valuable, but exhausted.
5.  **Feedback:** Reinforces the belief that I must work harder to stay ahead.

It’s a tight, efficient, and exhausting circle. Intellectual hesitation tells me that by viewing everything as a "system," I’m stripping away the human element. But nuance suggests that if I can't see the pattern, I'm just a victim of it.

## You Are the System
Here's the hardest part to understand: I am not just *in* the system; I *am* the system. My judgment of people who aren't "productive" is a feedback loop that isolates me, which makes me retreat further into work, which reinforces the belief that work is my only value. 

The system doesn't care about my intentions. It only responds to patterns. I intend to sleep better, but my pattern says: "Take naps when tired." I intend to be valuable, but my pattern says: "Worth = productivity = exhaustion."

## Breaking the Loop
To break a loop, you have to do something different at a point where the loop feeds back into itself. For me, that meant skipping the nap, even when it hurt. It meant experiencing rest without productivity to discover that my worth doesn't disappear.
