Today I was debugging a communication protocol for a new sensor, and I realized that my entire life has been a series of "firsts." As a first-born male in a Mexican family, I was the first to carry the weight of certain expectations. As a first-generation immigrant to Canada, and then to the United States, I was the first to navigate the complexities of a new culture, a new language, and a new identity. 

In engineering, we have a name for the first of something: **The Prototype.**

## The Pressure of the Version 1.0
Being a prototype is exhausting. You are the one who has to find the bugs so the later versions don't have to. You are the one who has to prove that the "system"—whether it's a family’s move to a new country or a new mechanical design—is actually viable. 

I remember the jarring transition of moving to the United States. I was a stranger in a strange land, grappling with a sense of being "other." In those moments, I felt like a beta-test of a system that wasn't quite ready for the field. There were times when I felt invisible, lost in a sea of faces that didn't look like mine. 

## Resilience as a Core Component
But in the face of these challenges, my family’s resilience shone through. We built a new home, piece by piece, not with bricks and mortar, but with shared memories and unwavering support. This is exactly how I approach a complex build now. You don't build the whole robot at once; you build it sub-system by sub-system. 

It appears likely that my identity as a "First" is what gives me the stomach for high-stakes engineering. When you've already had to rebuild your entire life in a new language, a shattered 3D print or a fried microcontroller doesn't seem so devastating. 

## The Duty to Give Back
As the first-born son, I’ve always felt a deep sense of responsibility to set an example. This sense of duty is what led me to the world of engineering. I don't see technology as a way to "get ahead"; I see it as a way to create equity. 

Being Mexican-American has given me a unique perspective on the world. I’ve learned to see the world not as a collection of separate identities, but as a rich tapestry of interconnected experiences. I want to use my "First" status to trailblaze a path for others. Whether it's mentoring international robotics teams from Belize or Angola, or designing assistive tech, I am driven by the belief that you only truly rise if you bring others along for the journey.

## Final Thought
Will I ever be "finished" navigating these firsts? Probably not. But I’ve learned to embrace the prototype mindset. I am no longer afraid of the bugs in the system. I am the researcher who is learning how to fix them.

Today, I’m not just a student or an immigrant. I am a builder who knows that the "Weight of Firsts" is actually the strength that holds the whole structure together.
