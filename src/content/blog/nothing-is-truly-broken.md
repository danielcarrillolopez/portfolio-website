Today I caught a faint scent of motor oil and sawdust while walking past a local repair shop, and I was instantly transported back to Mexico. To understand my approach to engineering, you have to understand my grandfather’s workshop. It wasn’t a "high-tech" lab with CNC mills or laser cutters. It was a dusty, crowded space filled with a lifetime of salvaged parts and a handful of well-worn tools. But it was there that I learned my first and most important engineering lesson: **Nothing is ever truly broken beyond repair.**

## The Materials Science of Resourcefulness
In that workshop, resourcefulness wasn't just a skill—it was a way of life. My grandfather could fix anything with a bit of ingenuity and whatever was on hand. He taught me that every "scrap" has value if you understand its properties. 

I remember helping him fix a broken irrigation pump. We didn't have the "correct" replacement seal, so we spent the afternoon fabricating one from a scrap of heavy-duty rubber and some hand-filed washers. It wasn't about following a manual; it was about asking, *"Why did this fail?"* and then iterating until it didn't. 

## Failure as a Conversation
My Mexican upbringing taught me that every failure contains information. Working with my grandfather, I learned that my tenth attempt at a fix would always be better than my first because each failure taught me something new about the system. 

In modern engineering, we call this **Rapid Prototyping** or **Agile Iteration**, but to me, it will always be the "Conversation with Failure." It’s an optimistic way of looking at the world. It suggests that a setback isn't a conclusion—it’s just a data point.

## The Immigrant’s Lab
This mindset was further refined when we immigrated to Canada and then to the United States. As a first-generation immigrant, you are constantly in "prototype mode." You are iterating on your language, your customs, and your identity. You learn to work with whatever "materials" you have rather than waiting for perfect conditions. 

It appears likely that my drive to specialize in **robotic prosthetics** comes directly from this workshop philosophy. If a person loses a limb, the world might see them as "broken." But I see a technical problem that requires a creative, human-centered solution. I want to build devices that don't just replace what was lost but restore the sense of self that comes with being whole.

## Final Reflection
Looking at my current projects like the **Synapse Haptic Band**, I see my grandfather’s influence in every solder joint. I’m not just building hardware; I’m continuing a legacy of resourcefulness. Intellectual hesitation tells me that I should aim for the most "advanced" technology, but nuance suggests that the best engineering is the kind that works when things are far from perfect.

Today, I’m not just looking for solutions. I’m looking for the "oil and sawdust"—the messy, real-world problems where ingenuity makes the difference.
